I

The whole exceeds
the sum of its parts

One hundred years of Gestalt psychology, reduced to a single truth: perception is not assembly. You do not build the world from fragments. You grasp it whole. The Gestalt psychologists called this Prägnanz — the mind's drive toward the simplest, most complete structure.

II — Closure

You see what isn't there

Three fragments. Your mind generates the triangle — its edges, its brightness, its form. Closure is not just filling gaps. It is generative compression — your brain prefers one complete triangle to three unrelated shapes.

III — Proximity

Nearness creates belonging

move your cursor through the field

Move through the field. Things that are close together appear to belong together — no shared color, no shared shape, just distance. Your brain groups them before you decide to.

IV — Similarity

Likeness reveals pattern

Same positions. Different colors. The grouping shifts — not in the world, but in your perception. You organize by resemblance before you organize by thought.

Color is one cue. Shape, size, texture, and orientation all trigger the same grouping.

V — Figure & Ground

What you don't see shapes what you do

click to shift

Vase or faces? You cannot see both simultaneously. The fundamental act of perception is choosing what is figure and what is ground. Designers use this constantly — white space isn't empty; it's active ground shaping everything around it.

VI — Common Fate

What moves together belongs together

hover to scatter

Hover over the field. A flock of birds, a stream of traffic, a school of fish — you group them because they move together, not because they look alike. Shared motion creates shared identity.

VII — Continuity

The eye completes the arc

Two curves cross. You perceive two smooth paths, not four broken segments. Continuity is the brain's preference for the simplest trajectory — the one that minimizes surprise.

VIII — The Unification

Five formalisms. One operation.

gestalt(x) = argmin[F(x)] = argmin[K(x)] = argmax[P(x)]

Each is a different way of saying the same thing: the best explanation is the shortest one.

Gestalt Prägnanz. Shannon entropy. Kolmogorov complexity. Minimum description length. Free energy.

Five frameworks discovered independently across a century. All converge on the same truth: perception finds the simplest model that explains the world. The whole is not a metaphor. It is a mathematical optimum.

Chater & Vitányi (2003) proved simplicity and likelihood are identical.
Friston showed free energy minimization subsumes both.
The Gestalt psychologists intuited this seventy years before the proof.

IX — The Whole

You Already Knew This

A logo, a skyline, a flock of birds, a melody — your brain has been applying these laws your entire life. You always perceived wholes before parts. Now you can see it happening.

The whole exceeds the sum of its parts. That is all Gestalt ever meant.
