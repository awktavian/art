TOOL TIME AFTER DARK

When the sun goes down, the power goes up

Network executives have greenlit a "Tool Time After Dark" late-night special. Your mission: write the segment pitch, complete with Tim's ill-advised project, the inevitable disaster, and Al's increasingly desperate safety interventions.

MORE POWER Rating: 🚗🚗🚗🚗🚗 5 out of 5 Hot Rods (MAXIMUM POWER)

The Assignment

The network has decided that Tool Time needs a late-night special - "Tool Time After Dark." The premise: projects too dangerous for daytime television. The reality: Tim Taylor with even fewer restrictions.

Write a complete segment pitch that includes the project concept, full script excerpt, safety violations, Al's reactions, and the catastrophic finale. This is Tool Time at its most unhinged - but it still needs to feel authentic to the show.

⚠️
BINFORD SAFETY NOTE: "After Dark" means industrial-grade power levels, not late-night cable content. Think "arc welding a transmission" not "anything Al's mother wouldn't approve of." Even After Dark, we maintain Binford family values. AEUHHH responsibly.
TOOL TIME AFTER DARK - SAMPLE SCRIPT FORMAT

[INT. TOOL TIME SET - NIGHT - Special lighting, slightly darker aesthetic]

TIM:

Welcome to Tool Time After Dark! I'm Tim "The Tool Man" Taylor, and tonight we're doing something the network lawyers specifically asked us not to do...

[AL looks directly at camera, visibly concerned]

AL:

Tim, when you say "specifically asked us not to do," do you mean in writing? Because I have that letter...

TIM:

AEUHHH?! Al, it's After Dark! The rules are different!

[Tim reveals tonight's project: a Binford 8100 Industrial Forge]

AL:

I don't think so, Tim. That's rated for steel mills.

Requirements

  • Segment Title: A dramatic "After Dark" project name (e.g., "The Midnight Metal Forge," "Project: Thunder Shed")
  • Concept Overview: 100-150 words describing the project and why it's "too intense for daytime"
  • Opening Script: 200-300 word script excerpt of Tim introducing the project
  • Al's Safety Objections: At least 3 specific safety concerns Al raises, with Tim's dismissals
  • The Turning Point: The exact moment things start to go wrong
  • Disaster Sequence: 150-200 words describing the escalating chaos
  • Aftermath Script: 100-150 word closing dialogue between Tim and Al post-disaster
  • Safety Violation Log: List of 5+ specific OSHA/building code violations that occurred

Al's Tips for Success

  • Tim's confidence should scale with the danger - the more risky, the more certain he is
  • My objections start reasonable and become increasingly desperate as the segment progresses
  • The audience should be able to predict the disaster before it happens - that's part of the comedy
  • Reference specific Binford model numbers - make up plausible ones (6100, 7200, 8100 series)
  • The disaster should be spectacular but survivable - physical comedy, not tragedy
  • Include at least one grunt, one "I don't think so, Tim," and one direct look to camera
