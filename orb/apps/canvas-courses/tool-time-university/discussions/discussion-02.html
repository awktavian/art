Discussion Prompt

Al Borland: Unsung Hero

"I don't think so, Tim." With those five words, Al Borland established himself as the voice of reason, safety, and competence on Tool Time — and by extension, in the entire Home Improvement universe.

Richard Karn's Al was originally meant to appear in just one episode. Instead, the flannel-wearing assistant became Tim's indispensable foil for all 204 episodes. But Al's role raises fascinating questions about how we value different kinds of contribution, particularly in professional and creative partnerships.

Al is demonstrably more skilled than Tim at nearly every aspect of their job. He's safer, more precise, more knowledgeable about proper technique. Yet Tim is the star, the one with the catchphrases and the audience love. Al gets the grunt work, the setup, and occasionally — the punchline at his expense.

Your Task

Write a 500-750 word character analysis exploring Al Borland's role as the "competent sidekick" and what his dynamic with Tim reveals about how we value different types of success. Consider the following analytical frameworks:

Character Analysis Framework

Examine Al through these lenses:

  • The Competence Paradox: Al is better at the job but will never be the star. What does this say about how society values charisma vs. capability?
  • Professional Identity: Al derives genuine satisfaction from his work while tolerating constant indignity. Is this admirable patience or unhealthy accommodation?
  • Loyal Opposition: "I don't think so, Tim" is both disagreement and support. How does Al balance correction with loyalty?
  • The Straight Man Theory: Comedy requires the setup as much as the punchline. Is Al's role actually more essential than Tim's?

Guiding Questions

Address at least three of the following in your essay:

  1. Why does Al stay? He has the skills to host his own show, run his own business, or work somewhere he'd be appreciated. What motivates his loyalty to Tim?
  2. Compare Al's situation to real-world dynamics: the reliable employee overlooked for promotion, the session musician behind the rock star, the ghostwriter. What patterns emerge?
  3. How does the show treat Al's competence? Is it celebrated, mocked, or simply taken for granted?
  4. Examine Al's personal life (his mother, his relationships, his flannel). How does his off-screen identity connect to his professional role?
  5. What would Tool Time be without Al? What would it be without Tim? Which loss would be more devastating?
  6. Does Al represent a healthier model of masculine identity than Tim? Defend your position.

Example

Sample Strong Response (Excerpt)

The following is an excerpt from a previous semester's exemplary submission:

Al Borland represents what organizational psychologist Adam Grant calls a "giver" in a world that rewards "takers." In "Al's Fair Lady" (Season 5, Episode 22), we see Al meticulously prepare a segment on proper joint-fitting technique, only to have Tim hijack the demonstration with increasingly powerful pneumatic equipment. The segment ends, predictably, with Al covered in sawdust and the audience in stitches. But watch Al's face in that moment: there's resignation, yes, but also something like affection. He knew this would happen. He prepared for it anyway.

This mirrors the dynamic between Steve Wozniak and Steve Jobs in the early Apple days. Wozniak was the technical genius; Jobs was the showman. History remembers Jobs because charisma scales in a way competence doesn't. When Wozniak built the Apple I, perhaps two hundred people appreciated its elegance. When Jobs sold it, millions wanted one. Al builds; Tim sells. The question Home Improvement poses but never quite answers is whether this division is fair, necessary, or simply the way humans organize around talent and personality.

In "Bachelor of the Year" (Season 3, Episode 16), Al finally receives external recognition when a magazine names him Detroit's most eligible bachelor. The episode treats this as comedy — Al? A sex symbol? — but there's a subversive edge. Al's qualities (reliability, kindness, actual skill) ARE attractive, but the show can only frame them through the lens of humor because sincerely celebrating them would undermine Tim's protagonist status...

— Fall 2023 Submission (used with permission)

This excerpt demonstrates specific episode knowledge, draws a compelling real-world parallel (Wozniak/Jobs), introduces academic framework (Adam Grant), and offers interpretive insight ("sincerely celebrating them would undermine Tim's protagonist status").
