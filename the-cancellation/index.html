The
Cancellation

a3 + b3 = c3

Fermat said no solution exists.
In four dimensions, one does.

The example

Three quaternion integers, each of norm 2. Their cubes satisfy the equation Fermat's Last Theorem forbids.

a = 1 + i    b = −1 + j    c = −i − j
 
a³  =  (1+i)²(1+i)  =  2i(1+i)  =  −2 + 2i
b³  =  (−1+j)²(−1+j)  =  −2j(−1+j)  =  +2 + 2j
 
a³ + b³  =  (−2 + 2i) + (+2 + 2j)  =  2i + 2j
real parts cancel: −2 + 2 = 0
 
c²  =  i² + ij + ji + j²  =  −1 + k − k − 1  =  −2
c³  =  −2(−i−j)  =  2i + 2j   ✓

Why it works

a lives in the complex plane ℝ+ℝi. b lives in a different one: ℝ+ℝj. They share only the real line. Their cubes have real parts of opposite sign. Those parts cancel.

Then c = −i−j needs c² to be real. It is — because ij = −ji. The cross terms in c² annihilate each other. That's anti-commutativity: swapping the order of multiplication flips the sign. It makes c² collapse to a number, and c³ proportional to c.

In ℤ, there is one direction. Real parts add. They never cancel. In four dimensions, independent imaginary directions anti-commute, and the cancellation is exact — for every odd power, not just cubes.

Anti-commutativity is the lock. Two imaginary directions are the key.

Since (1+u)² = 2u, the (2n+1)-th power is (2u)n·(1+u). For b = −1+v, the same gives (−2v)n·(−1+v). The "real" terms (2u)n and (−2v)n cancel for all n when u and v anti-commute. The imaginary parts combine into a power of −(u+v). This is proved for all n by induction in 22 kernel-verified lemmas.

The null results are as important as the positive ones. The Hurwitz quaternion integers have a 24-element unit group (vs 8 for Lipschitz), including ω = (1+i+j+k)/2. But ω³ = −1 — the cubing map collapses the richer unit group back to Lipschitz. No new families emerge.

In the octonions, 600,768 solutions were tested for the associator [a,b,c] = (ab)c − a(bc). Every single one had [a,b,c] = 0. The triple lives in an associative subalgebra, even when individual elements span 4–7 imaginary directions. Power-associativity and Artin's theorem (any two elements generate an associative subalgebra) force this: the cubing operation is "blind" to non-associativity.

The mechanism is exactly anti-commutativity. Nothing more. Nothing less.

The frontier

What does this construction open? The equation ap + bp = cp is a probe: it tests the arithmetic structure of a ring. The answers reveal how algebraic properties shape Diophantine behavior.

Waring's problem for quaternion cubes. In ℤ, every integer is a sum of 9 cubes (Vinogradov). In ℍℤ, Pollack (2018) proved every Lipschitz integer is a sum of finitely many cubes. The exact number g(3, ℍℤ) is unknown. Our solutions show that some cubes combine in unexpected ways — they can cancel as well as add.

Cross product arithmetic. The G&sub2; cross product a×b on ℤ7 satisfies the normed identity |a×b|² = |a|²|b|² − ⟨a,b⟩² — the same identity as in 3 dimensions (Adams 1960: only dimensions 3 and 7). The orbits of G&sub2;(ℤ) on cross product fibers may parametrize cubic rings, connecting to Bhargava's higher composition laws. This is an open conjecture.

The Langlands bridge. The four normed division algebras organize four levels of the Langlands program: class field theory (ℝ), modular forms (ℂ), quaternion inner forms (ℍ), exceptional automorphic forms (𝕆). FLT is proved at level 2 using level 3 machinery. The octonionic level 4 — G&sub2; modular forms, Fourier coefficients indexed by cubic rings, Selmer groups in the 14-dimensional adjoint — is the open frontier. No R = T theorem exists for G&sub2;. No Frey construction is known. The landscape is where GL&sub2; was in the 1970s.

Ring-specific Fermat. The real question this construction poses: for which rings R does ap + bp = cp have solutions? The answer depends on R having anti-commuting square roots of −1 (sufficient for odd p), on the norm form (even p reduces to FLT on norms), and on the unit group structure (Hurwitz units collapse under cubing). A systematic study of Fermat equations across algebraic habitats — matrix rings, Clifford algebras, p-adic division algebras, function fields — is legitimate and largely unexplored mathematics.

What it means

Impossibility theorems live inside a habitat. In ℤ, powers are trapped in a rigid arithmetic ecology — the kind that takes the Langlands program to map. In the quaternion integers, anti-commuting directions create a structural bypass. The same exponent goes from impossible to easy because the ambient algebra changed.

This does not simplify Wiles. It contextualizes him. Breaking Fermat in four dimensions takes one identity. Proving it can't break in one dimension took 109 pages. The asymmetry is the point: breaking things is easy; proving things can't break is hard.

A sharply illuminating counterexample to a naive extension of FLT, and a reminder that algebraic structure — not just exponent size — decides what equations can breathe.
