The Cancellation

a3 + b3 = c3

Fermat said this has no solution.
He was right — in one dimension.
In four, it does.

I

The Claim

In 1637, Fermat wrote in a margin that an + bn = cn has no whole-number solutions for n ≥ 3. In 1995, Andrew Wiles proved him right. The proof took seven years of solitary work and 109 pages of the deepest mathematics of the twentieth century.

But Fermat and Wiles worked in one dimension. The integers are a line.

In 1843, William Rowan Hamilton discovered the quaternions — a four-dimensional number system where multiplication doesn't commute. i · j = k, but j · i = −k. The order matters. Left is not right.

We asked: does Fermat's equation have solutions in four dimensions?

II

The Solution

a = 1 + i    b = −1 + j    c = −i − j

Three quaternion integers. Each has norm 2. Their cubes satisfy Fermat's equation exactly.

■   a³ + b³ = c³ = 2i + 2j
III

The Mechanism

a = 1 + i lives in one complex plane: ℝ + ℝ·i. b = −1 + j lives in a different complex plane: ℝ + ℝ·j. These two planes share only the real axis.

When you cube a, its real part is −2. When you cube b, its real part is +2. They cancel. Perfectly. Not just for cubes — for every odd power.

But why does c work? Because of anti-commutativity. In the quaternions, swapping the order of multiplication flips the sign: i·j = +k but j·i = −k. This matters when you square c:

c² = (−i − j)² = i² + i·j + j·i + j² = −1 + k − k − 1 = −2
i·j and j·i cancel — anti-commutativity makes c² a pure real number

Because ij = −ji, the cross terms destroy each other. c² collapses to the real number −2. Then c³ = −2 · (−i−j) = 2i + 2j — exactly what a³ + b³ produces.

Without anti-commutativity, c² would have an imaginary part, c³ wouldn't be proportional to c, and the whole mechanism falls apart.

In ordinary integers, there is only one direction. Both real parts point the same way. They add. They can never cancel. In four dimensions, independent imaginary directions that anti-commute create the room.

Anti-commutativity is the lock. Two imaginary directions are the key. Together they open the door that Fermat closed.
IV

The Proof

This works for all odd p ≥ 3. The entire proof is one identity.

Theorem   Non-Commutative Fermat
Let ei, ej be distinct imaginary units with ei² = ej² = −1. Set a = 1 + ei,   b = −1 + ej.   For all odd p ≥ 3:   Re(ap) + Re(bp)  =  2p/2 [cos(pπ/4) + cos(3pπ/4)]           =  2(p+2)/2 · cos(pπ/2) · cos(pπ/4)           =  0 cos(pπ/2) = 0 for all odd p. The real parts always cancel.

VII

What It Means

Fermat's Last Theorem is not a theorem about numbers. It is a theorem about commutativity.

In a world where ab = ba, the equation has no room. Every element lives on the same axis. The real parts of powers always point the same way. They add. They never cancel.

In a world where ab ≠ ba, there is room. Different imaginary directions are genuinely independent. Anti-commutativity makes cross terms destroy each other. The cancellation is exact, for every odd exponent, forever.

The proof is elementary — one trigonometric identity. The value is not in the difficulty. It is in the question, which apparently went unasked for 183 years. The result does not simplify Wiles' proof; it contextualizes it. Breaking Fermat in four dimensions takes one line of trigonometry. Proving it can't break in one dimension took seven years of solitary work and the deepest mathematics of the twentieth century.

The asymmetry is the point. Breaking things is easy. Proving things can't break is hard.

The margin was exactly wide enough for one dimension.
