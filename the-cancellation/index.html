The
Cancellation

a3 + b3 = c3

Fermat said no solution exists.
In four dimensions, one does.

The example

Three quaternion integers, each of norm 2. Their cubes satisfy the equation Fermat's Last Theorem forbids.

a = 1 + i    b = −1 + j    c = −i − j
 
a³  =  (1+i)²(1+i)  =  2i(1+i)  =  −2 + 2i
b³  =  (−1+j)²(−1+j)  =  −2j(−1+j)  =  +2 + 2j
 
a³ + b³  =  (−2 + 2i) + (+2 + 2j)  =  2i + 2j
real parts cancel: −2 + 2 = 0
 
c²  =  i² + ij + ji + j²  =  −1 + k − k − 1  =  −2
c³  =  −2(−i−j)  =  2i + 2j   ✓

Why it works

a lives in the complex plane ℝ+ℝi. b lives in a different one: ℝ+ℝj. They share only the real line. Their cubes have real parts of opposite sign. Those parts cancel.

Then c = −i−j needs c² to be real. It is — because ij = −ji. The cross terms in c² annihilate each other. That's anti-commutativity: swapping the order of multiplication flips the sign. It makes c² collapse to a number, and c³ proportional to c.

In ℤ, there is one direction. Real parts add. They never cancel. In four dimensions, independent imaginary directions anti-commute, and the cancellation is exact — for every odd power, not just cubes.

Anti-commutativity is the lock. Two imaginary directions are the key.

The boundary

Hurwitz (1898): there are exactly four normed division algebras. The first with anti-commuting square roots of −1 is the quaternions.

Scope   This table covers the four real normed division algebras. Commutative rings can violate Fermat-type equations by other mechanisms. What this construction isolates is one structural feature — anti-commuting square roots of −1 — in algebras with definite norm and no zero divisors. Even exponents ≥ 6 reduce to classical FLT on the norms; the phenomenon is specific to odd powers.

The proof

The cubic identity is proved symbolically in Lean 4 — in any ring with u² = v² = −1 and uv + vu = 0. No computation. Pure rewriting. Kernel-verified.

theorem symbolic_fermat_cubic
  {R : Type*} [Ring R] (u v : R)
  (hu : u * u = -1) (hv : v * v = -1)
  (hac : u * v + v * u = 0) :
  (1+u)*((1+u)*(1+u)) + (-1+v)*((-1+v)*(-1+v))
  = (-u-v)*((-u-v)*(-u-v))
0 sorry  ·  0 native_decide  ·  0 axioms  ·  kernel-checked

The all-odd-p extension: for a = 1+u and b = −1+v, the real parts of ap and bp sum to 2p/2[cos(pπ/4) + cos(3pπ/4)] = 2(p+2)/2·cos(pπ/2)·cos(pπ/4) = 0, since cos(pπ/2) vanishes for all odd p. This is elementary trigonometry, not formally verified.

What it means

Impossibility theorems live inside a habitat. In ℤ, powers are trapped in a rigid arithmetic ecology — the kind Wiles needed seven years and the Langlands program to map. In the quaternion integers, anti-commuting directions create a structural bypass. The same exponent goes from impossible to easy because the ambient algebra changed.

This does not simplify Wiles. It contextualizes him. Breaking Fermat in four dimensions takes one identity. Proving it can't break in one dimension took 109 pages. The asymmetry is the point.
