The Cancellation

a3 + b3 = c3

Fermat said this has no solution.
He was right — in the integers.
In the quaternion integers, it does.

I

The Example

In 1843, William Rowan Hamilton discovered the quaternions — a four-dimensional number system with three imaginary units i, j, k satisfying i² = j² = k² = −1. Unlike ordinary numbers, quaternion multiplication is not commutative: ij = k but ji = −k.

The Lipschitz integers are quaternions with integer coordinates: ℤ + ℤi + ℤj + ℤk. They form a ring — you can add, subtract, and multiply them, and the results are again Lipschitz integers. Here is a triple of Lipschitz integers whose cubes satisfy Fermat's equation:

a = 1 + i    b = −1 + j    c = −i − j
✓   a³ + b³ = c³   ·   N(a) = N(b) = N(c) = 2   ·   all three nonzero
Verification
a²  =  (1 + i)²  =  1 + 2i + i²  =  1 + 2i − 1  =  2i a³  =  2i · (1 + i)  =  2i + 2i²  =  −2 + 2i real part of a³ is −2 b²  =  (−1 + j)²  =  1 − 2j + j²  =  −2j b³  =  −2j · (−1 + j)  =  2j − 2j²  =  +2 + 2j real part of b³ is +2   a³ + b³  =  (−2 + 2i) + (2 + 2j)  =  −2 + 2 = 0  +  2i + 2j the real parts cancel — opposite signs from opposite subalgebras c²  =  (−i − j)²  =  i² + ij + ji + j²  =  −1 + k − k − 1  =  −2 anti-commutativity: ij = −ji, so cross terms cancel → c² is real c³  =  −2 · (−i − j)  =  2i + 2j   ✓
■   a³ + b³ = c³ = 2i + 2j
II

The Mechanism

a = 1 + i lives in the complex subalgebra ℝ + ℝi. b = −1 + j lives in a different copy: ℝ + ℝj. These two planes share only the real axis.

When you cube a, its real part is −2. When you cube b, its real part is +2. They cancel. The imaginary parts — which live in independent directions — combine freely.

Two structural features make this work:

Anti-commutativity: ij = −ji. When you square c = −i − j, the cross terms ij and ji are equal in magnitude and opposite in sign. They annihilate each other. This makes c² a pure real number (−2), which in turn makes c³ proportional to c itself.

Orthogonal subalgebras: a and b live in different complex planes. Their cubes have real parts with opposite signs because a = 1 + u and b = −1 + v have opposite real components, and the cube formula amplifies this opposition.

In the ordinary integers, there is only one direction. Both real parts point the same way. They add. They never cancel.

Anti-commutativity is the lock. Orthogonal imaginary directions are the key.
III

The General Case

The cubic example generalizes to all odd exponents. The real-part cancellation persists because of a trigonometric identity.

Theorem
Let u, v be pure imaginary quaternions with u² = v² = −1 and uv = −vu. Set a = 1 + u,   b = −1 + v. Then for all odd p ≥ 3:   ap = 2p/2(cos(pπ/4) + u·sin(pπ/4)) bp = 2p/2(cos(3pπ/4) + v·sin(3pπ/4))   Re(ap) + Re(bp) = 2p/2[cos(pπ/4) + cos(3pπ/4)]          = 2(p+2)/2 · cos(pπ/2) · cos(pπ/4) = 0 cos(pπ/2) = 0 for all odd p — the real parts always cancel   Setting cp = τp(−1)(p−1)/2(u + v), where τp = √2 · sin(pπ/4) ∈ {±1}: ap + bp = cp p   for all odd p ≥ 3.
■
Note

The sign of c depends on p (mod 8). For p = 3: c = −u − v. For p = 7: c = u + v. The same a and b work for all odd p, but c must be adjusted. The hypothesis requires anti-commutativity (uv = −vu), not merely distinctness.

IV

The Boundary

There are exactly four normed division algebras over the reals: dimensions 1, 2, 4, 8. This is Hurwitz's theorem (1898). The first algebra with two anti-commuting square roots of −1 is the quaternions.

For even exponents p ≥ 6, the equation reduces to Fermat's equation on the norms (ordinary integers), so FLT still applies. Even-power solutions don't exist. The phenomenon is specific to odd exponents.

Scope

The table is restricted to the four real normed division algebras. Commutative rings can also violate Fermat-type equations for other reasons (e.g., 1³ + 1³ = t³ in ℤ[t]/(t³−2)). What this construction isolates is one specific structural feature — anti-commuting imaginary directions — that enables solutions in a family of division algebras where the norm form is definite and no zero divisors exist.

VI

What It Means

Not that Fermat was wrong. Not that Wiles missed a loophole. And not that non-commutativity magically destroys all Fermat phenomena.

What it means is that impossibility theorems in arithmetic live inside a habitat. In ℤ, powers are trapped in a brutally rigid ecology — the kind that takes seven years and the Langlands program to fully understand. In Lipschitz quaternion integers, you suddenly have intersecting complex planes and anti-commuting directions whose squares collapse in just the right way. The same exponent goes from impossible to easy because the ambient algebra changed.

The construction isolates one structural feature absent from ℤ: anti-commuting square roots of −1. Their presence enables a cancellation that their absence forbids. The proof of that absence in ℤ is trivial. The proof that the absence of all possible escape routes in ℤ is sufficient to block Fermat solutions — that is Wiles' 109 pages.

This example does not simplify Wiles. It contextualizes him. Breaking Fermat in four dimensions takes one line of trigonometry. Proving it can't break in one dimension took seven years of solitary work and the deepest mathematics of the twentieth century. The asymmetry is the point.

Beautiful example. Real mathematics. Not a revolution.
Best read as a sharply illuminating counterexample to a naive extension of FLT, and a reminder that algebraic structure — not just exponent size — decides what equations can breathe.
VII

Prior Work

The terrain is not unexplored. Apel (1931) studied Fermat's equation in real quaternions. Ribenboim (2004) studied it over p-adic quaternion fields. Li and Yuan (2025, arXiv:2503.03621) studied Fermat's and Catalan's equations over 2×2 integer matrices, which contain the Lipschitz integers as a subring. Ward (2008) proved Fermat's cubic has only trivial solutions in ℤ[i].

This specific triple — with its explicit anti-commutativity mechanism and odd-exponent generalization — appears to be a fresh observation. But the broader question of Fermat equations in non-commutative algebras is established mathematical territory.
